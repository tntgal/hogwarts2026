A Beginner's Field Guide to Magical Brewing
Your Journey Begins · Summer 2026 · Book One
Before You Begin

In Chapter Eight of Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone, Professor Snape surveys his new first-year class and asks a question no one expects: "What would I get if I added powdered root of asphodel to an infusion of wormwood?" Harry doesn't know. Neither does anyone else.

But Snape's point is not about the answer. It is about something deeper — that potions are not just mixing things together. They are about precision, patience, and paying attention to what happens when ordinary ingredients become something extraordinary.

"I don't expect you will really understand the beauty of the softly simmering cauldron with its shimmering fumes, the delicate power of liquids that creep through human veins, bewitching the mind, ensnaring the senses..."

— Professor Severus Snape · Chapter Eight · The Potions Master

The three potions below can be brewed at home using things you already have. Each one is tied to something that happens in Book One. Name your potion, photograph it dramatically, and submit it to earn 10 House Points.

⭐ Beginner · Ages 6 and up · No heat required
Troll Bogey Repellent
Repello Stultorum Nasi
Keeps trolls at a safe distance · probably
Book One Connection — In Chapter Ten, a fully grown mountain troll is let loose in the dungeons of Hogwarts on Halloween. It ends up in the girls' bathroom, where Harry and Ron follow it to rescue Hermione. Ron successfully casts Wingardium Leviosa to levitate the troll's own club and knock it unconscious. The troll's distinguishing characteristic — aside from being twelve feet tall and smelling terrible — is an enormous quantity of bogeys. This potion was likely invented by Hogwarts groundskeepers shortly thereafter.
🫙 Ingredients
  • 🟢½ cupWhite school glue (Elmer's)
  • 💧¼ cupWater
  • 🎨3–5 dropsGreen food coloring
  • ✨1 pinchGreen or silver glitter
  • 🧂½ tspBaking soda
  • 🫧1 tbspContact lens solution (must contain boric acid)
📜 Instructions
  1. Pour glue into a bowl. Add water and stir until combined.
  2. Add green food coloring and stir until the potion turns a deep, murky swamp-green.
  3. Add glitter and baking soda. Stir thoroughly — this is your activation base.
  4. Add the contact solution one drop at a time, stirring constantly. Watch carefully — the potion will begin to solidify and pull away from the sides.
  5. When it holds together but is still gloriously slimy, it is ready. Knead briefly with your hands.
  6. Name your potion. Photograph it in the most dramatic way possible.
⚗️ Purpose & Properties

This potion — technically a magical slime — replicates the sticky, adhesive quality of troll bogeys, which are known to trap small woodland creatures and, according to unverified reports, slow charging trolls by approximately 4%. When applied to a doorstep, it allegedly causes any troll who steps in it to become confused and wander toward a different dungeon entirely. Results may vary. Not recommended for carpet.

🧙 Potions Master Note: The contact solution activates the slime through a chemical reaction with the boric acid. This is actual chemistry — not magic. (Or perhaps magic is just chemistry we haven't fully explained yet.) Adult supervision recommended for young brewers.
📸 For Your Submission: Stretch it, drip it dramatically, hold it up to a window for the light — and don't forget to name it. What exactly does yours repel?

Elixir of Erised
Potion of the Heart's Desire
Reveals what you truly want · if you dare to look
Book One Connection — In Chapter Twelve, Harry stumbles upon the Mirror of Erised hidden in an unused classroom. The mirror shows not your face, but your heart's desire. Its inscription — Erised stra ehru oyt ube cafru oyt on wohsi — reads backwards: I show not your face but your heart's desire. Dumbledore later tells Harry: "It does not do to dwell on dreams and forget to live." This potion is said to cause a shimmering vision to appear in still water — a reflection of something longed for. The vision fades in minutes. The wanting does not.
🫙 Ingredients
  • 💜1 cupWarm water
  • 🫧2 tbspWhite vinegar
  • 🎨5–8 dropsPurple food coloring
  • ✨1 tspSilver or purple glitter
  • 🫧1 tbspDish soap
  • 🧂2 tspBaking soda
  • 🌿1 dropVanilla extract (the scent of longing)
📜 Instructions
  1. Fill a tall clear glass or vase with warm water.
  2. Add purple food coloring and stir slowly — let it swirl and bloom before fully mixing.
  3. Add glitter and stir gently. Watch the light catch it.
  4. Add vanilla extract. Note the scent — Dumbledore once said the Mirror shows us what we most desire. What does yours smell like?
  5. In a separate small cup, mix dish soap and baking soda together.
  6. Pour the vinegar into the tall glass. Immediately add the baking soda mixture. The potion will erupt in a shimmer of purple foam.
  7. Lean over the foam. What do you see in it?
⚗️ Purpose & Properties

The Elixir of Erised is said to produce a shimmering purple foam in which the brewer can briefly perceive a vision of their deepest wish. Unlike the Mirror itself, which can trap a person in dreaming, the Elixir's effect lasts only as long as the foam — reminding the drinker (or viewer) that desire is powerful but fleeting. The vanilla base provides comfort. The eruption provides wonder. The glitter reminds you that some things only shine when everything is a little chaotic.

🧙 Potions Master Note: The vinegar-baking soda reaction produces harmless carbon dioxide gas — the same fizz in your sparkling water. The eruption is real chemistry. Pour over a tray or sink to catch overflow. Food coloring stains — protect your surfaces.
📸 For Your Submission: Photograph the moment of eruption if you can — it happens fast. A slow pour from above creates a beautiful layered effect. What did you see in the foam?

Snape's Logic Draught
The Potion Puzzle · Chapter Sixteen
One moves forward · one allows retreat · choose wisely
Book One Connection — In Chapter Sixteen, Hermione and Harry face Snape's challenge: seven bottles of varying shapes, each containing a different liquid. One is poison. One moves forward through the black fire. One allows retreat through the purple. The rest are nettle wine or useless. A riddle tells them which is which. Hermione solves it instantly. "This isn't magic," she says. "It's logic." This potion recreates those seven bottles as a density column — each liquid settling into its own layer, just as the bottles stood in a line, each containing something different. Your job is to layer them without mixing.
🫙 Ingredients — The Seven Bottles
  • 🍯2 tbspHoney (the heaviest — sinks)
  • 🔵2 tbspBlue corn syrup or colored corn syrup
  • 🍽️2 tbspDish soap (colored if possible)
  • 💧2 tbspWater + food coloring of choice
  • 🫒2 tbspVegetable oil
  • 🍶2 tbspRubbing alcohol + food coloring
  • 🟠2 tbspLamp oil or substitute: more vegetable oil + orange coloring
📜 Instructions
  1. Use a tall, narrow, clear glass or cylinder — the taller the better.
  2. Pour honey first. Let it settle completely before adding the next layer.
  3. Pour corn syrup very slowly down the side of the glass using a spoon.
  4. Add dish soap next, also poured gently down the side.
  5. Add the colored water. Pour it over the back of a spoon to slow the pour.
  6. Add vegetable oil slowly. Do not stir. Wait 30 seconds between each layer.
  7. Add rubbing alcohol last — pour it down the side using a spoon. Watch it float.
  8. Stand back. Seven layers. Each one distinct. Like the seven bottles in the chamber.
⚗️ Purpose & Properties

Snape designed his challenge not for strength or bravery, but for logic — knowing that most dark wizards are not equipped to reason carefully under pressure. The density column recreates his seven-bottle puzzle: each liquid occupies its own layer because of differences in density, just as each bottle in the chamber held something distinct. The lesson is the same as Hermione's: some of the most powerful magic in the world is simply paying attention. This potion does not move you forward or backward. But making it carefully, without rushing, and watching the layers settle — that is its own kind of magic.

🧙 Potions Master Note: Rubbing alcohol is flammable — keep away from heat sources. Lamp oil substitute (vegetable oil + coloring) works just as well and is completely safe. Do not drink this. The layers will slowly begin to mix after a few hours — photograph it soon after brewing.
📸 For Your Submission: Hold the glass up to a window or lamp so the light passes through the layers. Name each bottle — which one moves forward? Which allows retreat? Which are the nettle wine?
